Health: where the account stands today

Health = EWS_SCORE × 100. EWS is PagerDuty's pre-validated behavioral composite, read directly from the data source. It integrates six behavioral signals: seat deployment, active responders (90 days), escalation policies generating incidents each month, mobile notification reach, high-urgency incidents resolved by a human, and EP schedule coverage with incidents. No recomputation happens in this report.

Each account card shows three context panels that explain what is driving the EWS level. Adoption shows how broadly the product is deployed and configured. Engagement shows how actively teams are using the platform. Outcomes shows sentiment signals from customer communications. These panels are independent context, not inputs to the health score.

Green ≥ 55 Healthy behavioral signals Yellow 40–54 Below target, watch closely Red < 40 Low engagement, high risk

Momentum: which direction the account is heading

Momentum starts at zero and accumulates points from behavioral signals. The result is a label: Positive (≥ +2 pts), Stable (between −2 and +2), or Negative (≤ −2 pts).

Deployment signals look at the 12-month OLS slope of seat deployment and the 3-month rolling window change, rewarding growth and penalising contraction. Incident volume uses OLS regression with a statistical significance gate (|t| ≥ 2) so noise does not score, plus an EWMA to confirm whether recent activity is above or below the account's own baseline. EWS trend signals fire when the EWS score itself is on a statistically significant sustained decline, or has been persistently in the Red tier for six or more consecutive months. Sentiment signals penalise low absolute sentiment and score the direction of change, so deterioration shows up even before hitting the low threshold. Pricing friction penalises active negative pricing language and escalates if it persists historically. ARR downgrades in non-renewal months add further penalty.

Competitive risk is the strongest signal in the entire model. When a competing incident management tool is detected in a customer’s environment, it immediately forces a Negative momentum classification and adds 30 priority points, overriding every other signal. No other signal works this way. It is treated as a hard override because a competitor being installed is the clearest early indicator that a customer is evaluating an exit, even when their health score still looks fine.

The combined result is a Positive, Stable or Negative trajectory label. Because some signals feed both dimensions, a customer in decline will show warning signs in both Health and Momentum. Momentum will surface them first, because it measures the rate of change before the current level has visibly deteriorated.

The four action groups

Expand: Green health (EWS ≥ 55), stable or positive momentum. The customer is realizing value and behavioral signals are not declining. Focus on growth: run expansion business reviews and introduce unconfigured capabilities.

Protect: Green health, negative momentum. The customer looks healthy today but key signals are moving in the wrong direction. Act now before the health score follows: freeze upsell motions and diagnose the root cause immediately.

Nurture: Yellow or Red health (EWS < 55), stable or positive momentum. Below target but not deteriorating. Run value reviews, close adoption gaps, and work to move them into Green.

Needs Attention: Below healthy with negative momentum, or at-risk due to routing overrides (health below 35, Red health with any declining signal, or direct competitor detected). Escalate to leadership, freeze all commercial motions, and put resources on removing whatever is blocking the customer.

What to think when a customer is unhealthy

Always look at momentum first. An unhealthy customer with stable or positive momentum is a recovery in progress. They are in a difficult place but not getting worse, and the right support can move them up. An unhealthy customer with negative momentum requires immediate action: they are sliding and the window to act is closing. Health tells you the severity. Momentum tells you the urgency. A competitor signal overrides both and demands an immediate response, regardless of everything else.

Health tiers (55 Green / 40 Yellow) and momentum thresholds are calibrated against the Nov 2025–Jan 2026 churned account retrospective (503 accounts). The framework detected 80% of churned accounts before churn. The remaining 20% fall into three structural categories: insufficient history (≤4 months), genuinely healthy at churn (external drivers such as M&A or budget), and chronic low adoption with no behavioral decline. Health = EWS_SCORE × 100. It is a single number read directly from the data source. EWS is PagerDuty's pre-validated behavioral composite; this report does not recompute it. Health answers: where does this account stand right now?

Momentum starts at zero and accumulates points from 11 independent signal groups. It answers: which direction is this account heading? Four of those signals are EWS time-series signals (they measure whether the EWS score itself is declining, dropping suddenly, or stuck in the Red tier). The remaining seven signal groups are completely independent of EWS: seat deployment velocity, incident volume trend, sentiment level, sentiment direction, pricing friction, ARR downgrades, and competitor presence. This means an account can have a stable EWS score but still show Negative momentum if, for example, sentiment is deteriorating or a competitor has been detected.
