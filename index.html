Health: where the account stands today

Health answers one question: is this customer getting real value from the product right now? It combines three pillars into a single score from 0 to 100.

Adoption measures how broadly the product is in use: the share of licensed seats actively deployed and how many of the 8 available modules the customer has activated. Engagement measures how actively their teams use the platform day to day: the share of responders active in the last 90 days and the share of escalation policies generating incidents each month. Outcomes measures the health of the relationship: primarily customer sentiment drawn from real emails, calls and support tickets, with NPS as a secondary signal. NPS carries limited weight because respondents are typically individual contributors, not the decision-makers who control renewals.

The three pillars are weighted by tenure. A new customer is judged mostly on adoption. A customer of three or more years is judged mostly on outcomes, because by that stage the product should be embedded and the relationship should reflect it.

Green ≥ 60  Value realized, low churn risk Yellow 40–59  Moderate value, needs attention Red < 40  Low value, high churn risk

Momentum: which direction the account is heading

Momentum answers a different question: is this account improving, stable or declining? It is not a time-over-time version of Health. Both dimensions use some of the same signals, but each also has signals that belong to it alone, and the ones they share are used in completely different ways.

Signals unique to Health include module breadth (how many of the 8 capabilities are active), active responder rate over the last 90 days, and NPS. Signals unique to Momentum include total incident volume trend over 25 months, pricing friction (a flag that fires when customer communications contain negative language about PagerDuty pricing), competitive risk (whether a rival incident management tool has been detected in the customer’s environment), and a 6-month window comparison that is currently in validation. The 6-month signal compares average activity over the last 6 months against the 6 months prior, asking whether the account is getting worse recently rather than whether it is low relative to its entire history. It fires only in the negative direction and is pending seasonal validation before inclusion in live scoring. These signals have no equivalent in the other dimension.

Signals shared between both dimensions are seat deployment rate, escalation policy activity and customer sentiment. Seat deployment and escalation policy activity work the same way in both: Health reads their current level, while Momentum reads the direction of change over time, asking whether each is growing or shrinking. Sentiment is used differently: both Health and Momentum read it as a current level, but they apply it differently. In Health it contributes to the Outcomes pillar score. In Momentum it acts as a friction signal, where a low score directly penalises the momentum rating. Same signal, applied to a different question.

Competitive risk is the strongest signal in the entire model. When a competing incident management tool is detected in a customer’s environment, it immediately forces a Negative momentum classification and adds 30 priority points, overriding every other signal. No other signal works this way. It is treated as a hard override because a competitor being installed is the clearest early indicator that a customer is evaluating an exit, even when their health score still looks fine.

The combined result is a Positive, Stable or Negative trajectory label. Because some signals feed both dimensions, a customer in decline will show warning signs in both Health and Momentum. Momentum will surface them first, because it measures the rate of change before the current level has visibly deteriorated.

The four action groups

Expand: Green health, positive or stable momentum. The customer is realizing value and not declining. Focus entirely on growth: introduce unconfigured modules and run expansion-focused business reviews.

Protect: Green health, negative momentum. The customer looks healthy today but key signals are moving in the wrong direction. Act now before the health score follows: freeze upsell motions and diagnose the root cause immediately.

Nurture: Yellow or Red health, stable or positive momentum. The customer is not where they need to be, but they are not getting worse. Run value reviews, close adoption gaps, and work to move them into Green.

Needs Attention: Yellow or Red health, negative momentum. Below healthy and actively declining. Escalate to leadership, freeze all commercial motions, and put resources on removing whatever is blocking the customer.

What to think when a customer is unhealthy

Always look at momentum first. An unhealthy customer with stable or positive momentum is a recovery in progress. They are in a difficult place but not getting worse, and the right support can move them up. An unhealthy customer with negative momentum requires immediate action: they are sliding and the window to act is closing. Health tells you the severity. Momentum tells you the urgency. A competitor signal overrides both and demands an immediate response, regardless of everything else.

Health tiers (60/40) and momentum thresholds are empirically set for this cohort and have not yet been validated against historical churn data. They are operationally useful guides, not statistically proven cutoffs. Health and Momentum share three signals: seat deployment rate, escalation policy activity and customer sentiment. For seat deployment rate and escalation policy activity, Health reads the current level while Momentum reads the direction of change over time. For sentiment, both dimensions read the current level but apply it differently: as a pillar score in Health, and as a friction penalty in Momentum. Each dimension also has signals exclusive to it: module breadth, active responder rate and NPS belong to Health only; incident volume trend, pricing friction and competitive risk belong to Momentum only. A fourth Momentum signal, the 6-month window comparison, is in validation and is currently surfaced as a monitoring flag only, not included in live scoring. The EWMA threshold is set at -20% for high-value renewal accounts, an interim empirical calibrationting for high-value renewal accounts. NPS has limited predictive value for renewal risk since respondents are typically individual contributors, not decision-makers. For full scoring rules see the framework reference document. Validating all thresholds against historical churn and downgrade outcomes is the recommended next step before making large-scale resource allocation decisions based on this framework.
